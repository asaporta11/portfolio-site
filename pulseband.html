Challenge

I designed and developed Pulseband: a data visualization tool empowering users to prioritize which lifestyle changes would be most impactful in lowering their risk for a cardiac event.

This was an exploratory project in which my task was to identify where to focus the Pulseband product, not brand it. For example, the Pulseband name was already specified as was the fact that it was a wearable device. Therefore, I was given the freedom to make decisions about the functionality of the product. Though this project was assigned to everyone in my cohort, I sought a dual focus on UX and development so I worked alone in a sprint environment.

Synthesis

Interviews taught me that understanding one’s own complex matrix of nutrition, physical activity, and preexisting conditions left patients feeling overwhelmed. Patients were unsure about which risk factors to address first, leaving them paralyzed with how to proceed. For example, if patients had high blood pressure due, in part, to high cholesterol and blood sugar, they were unsure about where to start: blood sugars or cholesterol? They also struggled with recognizing small improvements in the early stages of habit change.

This combination of paralysis and difficulty in recognizing small improvements lead to failure to implement and sustain new strategies. My goal in creating the pulseband product was to address this problem.

Product Design

Since user research revealed that users, i.e. patients, are often discouraged by an overwhelming amount of information and experience difficulty recognizing that small changes can have a large impact in the long term, I began the product design process with the goal to address these barriers to change.

Design Principles

I created a set of design principles that guided my problem solving process so that I could build a tool that would effectively empower patients to begin and sustain habit change.

Educate

users so they come away with a better understanding than they had before: help them understand that many different factors contribute to heart health, yet help them avoid getting lost in the details.

Motivate

users by helping them notice small changes and encourage them to stick with positive modifications.

Balance

users’ self-knowledge with their doctor’s recommendations; encourage exploration with Pulseband yet emphasize that their doctor is best for synthesizing all their information.

Synthesis

The process of defining these principles helped me prioritize the most important issues Pulseband had to address and understand how to approach each problem. Consequently, this helped me focus and refine my solutions during the ideation process.

Ideate

Guided by the design principles established above, I entered the ideation phase with the objective to ensure the tool I created would be valuable to users.

Educate: Data Visualization

User research revealed that patients have a difficult time understanding how heart health involves a complex interdependence of factors (cholesterol, sugars, weight loss, blood pressure, etc) so I needed to find a user-friendly way to represent them. Establishing the educate design principle helped me recognize that data visualizations can be an effective way of showing how multiple factors interact. This, in turn, could be modeled as an educational experience as long as the graph was designed in a user-friendly way.

I started with a line graph concept that indicated which factors correlated together, but this made the data seem daunting.

Since graphs can be overwhelming, I turned to cognitive psychology to understand how to best represent data in a user-friendly way. To make effective visualizations, I needed to understand how the brain processes visual information from the perspective of attention, memory, and understanding scale. Therefore, I conducted a literature review to better understand what makes for optimal learning conditions in a data visualization environment.

We have limits on our attention and working memory. Therefore, superfluous images distract from interpretation, so the graph must be simple.

Simulation: connects a current state of health with a potential future state. This helps users envision what their future health situation might look like if they make certain health changes in their current state.

Instant Feedback: critical to the learning process. Motion and color changes are forms of feedback. For example, colors can indicate healthy or unhealthy and graphical movement upon user interaction amplifies user engagement.

Users are presented with a graph of their current data. Since the graph is interactive, dragging a bar simulates the user’s potential future state of health.

Synthesis

Understanding the cognitive factors that influence our ability to interpret and learn from graphs directed my focus to which visual attributes of the Pulseband product were most important to promote user engagement.

Motivate: Small changes affect the big picture

Using the motivate design principle as a guide, I needed to find a way to help users take ownership of their health while encouraging positive, sustainable change. Therefore, I used simulation as a way to connect a user’s current health state with a potential future state. Forging this connection should be easily accessible and intuitive.

How can users assess their overall health in an informative and accurate metric? From my literature review, I found that the American Heart Association gauges patients’ susceptibility to a cardiac event in the form of % risk. Because this boiled the varied and complex factors that affect heart health into a single output, I incorporated it so that users could come away from the experience with a succinct message about their overall health state.

The % Risk bar helps users understand how small changes in daily habits affect their overall susceptibility to a cardiac event.

Balance: Connect to medical professionals

Help users understand Pulseband as a projection of their health which can help them start a discussion about health goals with their doctor. An important feature of the Pulseband is that it has data on the age, height and weight of the user and the wearable nature of the product enables it to take measurements of factors related to heart health. Since this assignment gave us license to create fictitious features, this extends to cholesterol and blood sugar measures, though this technology isn’t on the market yet.

Therefore, the graph will come pre-populated with users’ data for each metric (blood pressure, heart rate, physical activity, cholesterol, blood sugars, and BMI). This way, users can simulate a future health state based off their current state.

Rapid Prototyping & Usability Testing

Knowing that the prototyping process needed to incorporate interactivity to overcome the pain point of making data visualizations accessible and informative, I learned that some of the most informative prototypes could be low-fidelity. With a short timeline and development looming, paper prototyping was the most effective way to glean pain points and identify solutions.

This prototype made it possible for me to identify pain points in interactivity. For example, users struggled with understanding the healthy range sections for each metric.

In the above prototype, users were confused about the healthy range sections. Knowing that this wasn't intuitive enough for the user, I recognized that an interactive component of the graph needed to include the bars changing colors based on if the value was low, healthy, above average, or dangerous.

I made this prototype in Illustrator to demonstrate that bar color changes based on the value the bar represents (low, healthy, above average, dangerous).

Summary

Prototyping validated some of my intutions and taught me how to listen to users closely to help me make the experience of using the product innately understandable.
